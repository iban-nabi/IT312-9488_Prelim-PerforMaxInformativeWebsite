The Gallup Framework: A Modern System to Effective Performance Development

by: L'alouette

This article is a summary of the research paper, “Re-Engineering Performance Management” by Gallup, Inc. All the data presented here is sourced from this paper, as well as the references that the paper based its research on.




Recent findings from meta-analyses and company surveys found that the traditional approaches to performance management do not effectively motivate employees. This is because organizations have come to prioritize performance measurement over development. While measurement still matters, it needs to be reframed and designed to support development and performance improvement. There needs to be a philosophical and functional shift away from performance management and into performance development.


In a framework proposed by (Wigert & Harter, 2017) under Gallup, Inc., performance management has been re-engineered in order to structurally reform and incorporate the finest practices of evidence-based management, starting with making performance the subject of regular conversations that foster meaningful and successful relationships.
This framework embodies the shift from performance management to performance development via three core principles: establish expectations, continually coach, and create accountability. When performance becomes focused on these core principles, managers and employees can interact and form discussions that are encouraging, purposeful, and rewarding, which helps employees with their own performance, development, and career.


  1. Establish Expectations
    Only one in two employees clearly know what is expected of them when they go to work every day. One of the major flaws of traditional performance management is that it has managers using ratings and rankings that are often too vague and generalized (often using a simple 1-to-5 scale). They do not specify the actions, moments, and results that define performance, and therefore not reflect what the manager expects from each employee. It does not describe the impact or value that employees bring to the company, which leaves them feeling defensive and unappreciated. Gallup describes 3 key criteria when establishing expectations:
    • Clear
      Both the manager and the employee must be clear on which duties the employee is responsible for. Managers must help employees prioritize an employee’s workload so that they have a shared understanding of work expectations. This also makes the manager take co-ownership in how work gets done and can help adjust priorities in cases of conflicting demands. The manager may consider which work is more important, where the employee has inefficiencies, and whether staffing levels are appropriate for the work at hand.
    • Collaborative
      People tend to find goals more fair and motivating when they have a voice in setting them, according to multiple Gallup studies and meta analyses by (Cawley et al., 1998) and (Colquitt et al., 2001). This also ensures a mutual understanding that the goals they set are fair, relevant, and challenging.
    • Aligned
      The work that individuals perform must be aligned with the purpose and goals of their team and organization. When employees understand the purpose and strategy of the company, it allows them to develop personal objectives while keeping the company’s areas of focus in mind. This leads to their work ultimately having a greater impact within the company.
  2. Continually Coach

    The annual review is the biggest criticism of the traditional performance management process. Performance reviews in general are far too infrequent (48% of employees who answered Galllup’s survey receive performance reviews once a year), which leaves managers struggling to recall all the details of an employee’s performance. This often leads to over-reliance on basing reviews on an employee’s more recent actions, or by only remembering an employee’s negative behaviors within the past year. However, an employee’s behavior can change on a day-to-day basis and, depending on the demands at the time, may not be representative of their performance as a whole. Additionally, by the time feedback is shared during an annual review, it will be way too late to implement these changes into an employee’s behavior.
    Managers must implement consistent coaching when engaging their employees. This creates an open conversation about performance and makes employees more receptive to feedback. This puts them in an environment that provides everyday opportunities to learn, since coaching should be an ongoing process and not just a yearly event. Gallup recommends three key principles for effective coaching conversations:

    • Frequent

      Managers must interact with their employees consistently, whether it be through email, phone calls, or casual conversation. Employees do not need their manager looking over their shoulder while they work, but they will appreciate that their superior genuinely cares about them and their growth. They can be confident that they have someone to turn to for support, leading to better performance in the workplace.
      Data collected by a Gallup survey shows that, when managers provide daily feedback (versus annual feedback), their employees are:
      3.0 times more likely to strongly agree that they receive meaningful feedback
      3.6 times more likely to strongly agree that they are motivated to do outstanding work
      3.0 times more likely to be engaged at work
    • Focused
      Managers must be intentional and clear when assigning tasks and setting priorities. Coaching conversations have the danger of creating unnecessary stress in employees when they are too confusing, conflicting, and without clear direction.
    • Future-Oriented
      Traditional performance management gave reviews that focused on the past and what employees did wrong. A study by (Budworth et al., 2015) showed that when feedback focuses too narrowly on past mistakes and feels especially negative, employees feel demotivated and often perform worse. Thus, a better option is to have conversations about how employees can do their best work going forward. Focus on proactively planning on how to achieve future expectations, while also integrating feedback from an employee’s recent performance.
  3. Create Accountability
    After effectively establishing expectations and continually coaching employees, the desired performance outcomes become possible only if people are held accountable. Gallup recommends at least two formal progress reviews per year (as opposed to one annual review). This recommendation stems from Gallup’s research finding that only less than half of employees (40%) strongly agree that their manager holds them accountable to their performance goals. Since employees are already familiar with their manager’s expectations of them and receive consistent coaching in the workplace, these progress reviews are more of a formality and serve as a snapshot, or formal documentation, of an employee’s performance. In order to make these progress reviews more effective, Gallup has identified three key attributes:
    • Achievement-Oriented
      Progress reviews should firstly recognize and celebrate the achievements of an employee, whether they are large or small. This encourages the employee to stick to their personal performance goals and their willingness to learn new skills. Focusing on a vision for success makes it easier to understand opportunities for improvement while identifying barriers preventing employees from utilizing their full potential.
    • Fair & Accurate
      Managers and leaders must fundamentally change how they gather data for performance reviews if they expect to deliver fair and accurate feedback to employees. Gallup recommends basing performance measurement around three types of data:
      • Qualitative metrics which are within an employee’s control, and directly affects one’s productivity, profitability, accuracy, or efficiency.
      • Subjective observations that qualitatively reflect performance in terms of expectations.
      • Individualized goals that take into account each employee’s unique expertise, experiences, and job responsibilities, alongside the general responsibilities of the job.
      Finally, collecting performance data from multiple sources, such as managers, peers, and customers, helps build a well-rounded picture of the employee’s performance.
    • Developmental
      Progress reviews must ultimately have the conversation of building progress toward developmental goals. Managers are responsible for helping employees realize and meet their developmental goals, as this directly influences an employee’s personal and professional success. By gaining a better understanding of where employees want to go in their career and in their life, managers can better determine new expectations and priorities for these employees.
